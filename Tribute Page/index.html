Honoring Dr. Norman Borlaug

A pioneer whose work reshaped agriculture and saved millions from hunger.

Timeline of His Contributions:

  • 1914 – Born in the farming town of Cresco, Iowa.
  • 1933 – Enrolls at the University of Minnesota during the Great Depression.
  • 1935 – Works with the Civilian Conservation Corps, witnessing firsthand the power of food aid.
  • 1937 – Graduates and starts work with the U.S. Forestry Service.
  • 1938 – Marries Margret Gibson and returns to school to study plant diseases.
  • 1942 – Earns a Ph.D. in plant pathology and genetics.
  • 1944 – Travels to Mexico to lead a project focused on improving wheat production.
  • 1945 – Develops a method for growing wheat year-round, boosting harvests.
  • 1953 – Creates a wheat strain that's disease-resistant and thrives in varied climates.
  • 1960s – Introduces high-yielding wheat to South Asia, helping avert mass famine.
  • 1970 – Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his contributions to global food security.
  • 1980s – Supports agricultural programs in Africa, focusing on staple crops like maize and sorghum.
  • 2005 – Advocates for the responsible use of genetically modified crops to combat hunger.
  • 2009 – Passes away at age 95, leaving behind a legacy of scientific service to humanity.

"Norman Borlaug was a quiet, determined force who turned science into sustenance for the world."

— Statement from the United Nations
